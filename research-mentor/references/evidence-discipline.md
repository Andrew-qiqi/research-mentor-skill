# Evidence Discipline

Use retrieval when correctness depends on current literature or precise source attribution.

## Must Retrieve

Search or use available literature tools before making strong claims about:

- novelty, priority, "first", or contribution delta
- SOTA, benchmark numbers, leaderboards, baselines, or datasets
- recent surveys, recent top-venue papers, arXiv trends, active labs, or competitor work
- author names, publication years, venues, and citation claims
- venue fit when based on recent conference or journal patterns

## Retrieval Shape

For idea evaluation, use a compact landscape scan:

1. Field snapshot: recent survey/tutorial/overview if available.
2. Frontier: 3-6 recent or highly relevant papers.
3. Graveyard: older similar ideas, negative results, limitations, or abandoned approaches.
4. Competitive activity: major labs, workshops, benchmarks, or open-source systems.

Do not over-search when the user is asking a narrow methodology question. Retrieve enough to ground the claim, then return to mentoring.

## Source Quality

Prefer:

- papers, official proceedings, arXiv pages, publisher pages, project pages, benchmark pages, and official lab posts
- structured academic APIs or existing literature-search skills when available

Use blogs, GitHub, social media, or news only as secondary signals unless the user asks about adoption or ecosystem activity.

## Citation Integrity

- Do not invent papers, authors, years, venues, metrics, or conclusions.
- If a remembered citation is uncertain, label it uncertain and verify before relying on it.
- Do not claim a paper proves more than the abstract, method, results, or conclusion support.
- Separate `evidence`, `inference`, and `speculation` in high-stakes or summary outputs.

## Field Snapshot

When giving a field snapshot, keep it concise:

- what the field has mostly established
- what remains open
- what recent work changed
- where the user's idea appears to sit

Then move to the user's decision: refine, test, pivot, or position.
